5–10 years (1993–1998)
Intense focus on keyboard, preference for solitude, advanced academic performance, and asthma-related episodes.

Until the age of nine, I had birthday parties each year, but I often found them overwhelming. I would cry during surprise parties, shout, and become distressed. I remember one birthday when I was taken to someone else's house and saw people waiting to clap and sing “Happy Birthday.” I did not cry because of the celebration itself, but because there were many people and I strongly disliked surprises. When I turned nine, I asked my mother to stop organizing parties and instead give me a keyboard. From that point onward, the birthday parties stopped.

I received a keyboard and taught myself to play. I spent countless hours alone in my bedroom practicing, a pattern that continued until I was 18 years old. I made some friends and occasionally played football, but I was rarely chosen for teams. As a result, I often returned to playing alone.

I frequently fell off my bicycle and still have a scar on my foot from one of those accidents.

I had a childhood friend named Adriel who lived next door, but his closest friend was Bruno. They watched Dragon Ball Z, which I did not. I missed many common childhood experiences such as cartoons, television shows, and video games, and participated very little in them. I stood out academically for having high grades and often studied material in advance, before it was covered in class.

Around age 10, I remember hearing voices. During this period, I had asthma and was once hospitalized after nearly suffocating at the beach. My father helped calm me, and we arrived safely at the hospital. One memory that stands out is when a child at a local corner shop told me he wished he had the same Portuguese vocabulary as me while we were eating crisps. I felt extremely proud and validated that day.

At night, the nebuliser was always prepared for me. Around 3 a.m., I would wake up and quietly go to the living room to use it. The mist eased the tightness in my chest, allowing me to return to sleep. These nights felt long and silent, but the routine provided a sense of relief and control.

Sometimes, I felt afraid of the toys while walking to the nebuliser and needed to wake one of my parents to stay with me briefly. Occasionally, my sister or brother would go instead. Even now, I still recall my mother’s voice saying, “He woke up,” when I wake during the night or early morning, as she used to say this when I had asthma episodes. At the time, it was referred to as “nervous asthma.”

When I wake up, sounds initially seem significantly louder than normal, approximately two to three times more intense. Over a few minutes, this sensation gradually decreases. The same occurs with bright white lights. I remember shouting to have lights turned off and often using the nebuliser in the dark due to light sensitivity.

I was originally left-handed but was trained to use my right hand. I still predominantly use my left leg. I was frequently instructed to look at the camera and smile during photographs. Even during my British citizenship ceremony and other formal events, I experienced difficulty with photographers, who sometimes perceived me as rude due to my discomfort and difficulty following these social expectations.

10–15 years (1998–2003)
Anxiety and panic episodes; sensory overload; social difficulties; intense ideological interests; use of music for regulation.

During this period, I became strongly influenced by rebellion and anarcho-punk readings. One book led me to several others, and these ideas shaped much of my early adolescence. At school, I sometimes played unusual pranks and spoke very directly, which caused social conflicts and loss of friendships. At Mazarelo School, I once threw a chair at another student. On another occasion, I left a note on a teacher’s desk saying “Happy Teacher’s Day,” with an offensive word written inside. This resulted in being sent to the principal’s office.

I later transferred to Anglo Líder School, where I began experiencing significant anxiety and panic attacks, including sweating, dizziness, and fatigue. I frequently left school before classes began. I believe these crises were related to traveling alone to school in a noisy city environment. I preferred specific buses based on their sound characteristics.

In 1998, my parents traveled to Atlanta and brought me a Discman. I carried it daily, despite its weight, as a way to block out street noise. I listened to music continuously until the teacher entered the classroom, using it as a coping mechanism for sensory overload.

The school administration referred me to a psychologist for assessment. She showed me pictures of butterflies, lions, and other images. I did not fully understand the purpose of the evaluation and have not researched it since. Due to prolonged absences related to anxiety and panic attacks, I failed an entire academic year. Classmates referred to me as “tourist” or “the mute kid.” I frequently daydreamed, and when I returned to awareness, I would sometimes notice others laughing. Panic episodes could occur in various settings, including the street, bus, underground, shopping mall, or during family meals. I sought enclosed and quiet spaces, most commonly bathrooms, to regulate myself.

The school administration requested that I transfer to another school. My mother refused, stating that she had three children enrolled there and would have to remove all of us. It was a low-cost private school. During one episode, I told a history teacher to “f.off” in front of the class. The context was that classmates were being loud after break, and when the teacher entered shouting loudly at the class to be quiet, I reacted by shouting back. I believe I was highly stressed and overwhelmed at the time.

Common triggers included prolonged unwanted conversations, loud environments, and general sensory overload. During some panic episodes, I heard voices of my parents similar to those from my childhood asthma episodes. These voices were not harmful or commanding but appeared linked to memory. During this time, I developed strong auditory skills by playing music by ear. I never learned to read sheet music.

As a child, I cut my parakeets’ wings because I did not understand that it would hurt them. One of them bled significantly, which caused me intense guilt. I cared for it until it healed and later released all of them. I never repeated this behavior. I preferred keeping fish and spent long periods observing them in the aquarium. During childhood, I felt a sense of identification with them.

Holidays and flights

Travel routines, EasyJet Luton–Lisbon, A319/A320.

In 2024, during my holidays in Lisbon, my sister organized a BBQ for my birthday and invited everyone from the church as well as her personal friends. I wasn’t expecting this, as I had told them I just wanted my family there. I spent about 30 minutes at the party before leaving, using the excuse of taking an elderly neighbor, my Portuguese neighbor who was also there, home. When I travel, I usually fly EasyJet from Luton to Lisbon. I know the aircraft types (A319/A320), recognize crews, and even choose specific seats to sleep in. I have taken around 150 flights on this route. I notice patterns that others don’t. For example, I can tell when different pilots approach Lisbon differently: some make the correct turn over Costa da Caparica and land smoothly, while others turn too far left and only correct just before landing, which feels rough. I also notice differences on other routes, such as whether the approach goes over the Seine River delta in Normandy. I actually memorized the whole flight back and forth and the details. I am very sensitive to descent. Because of sinus issues, I can usually tell the plane is descending before the pilot announces it, both from the sound and from the direction of turns (e.g. crossing the Channel). I also notice crew routines. On the last flight of the day, they usually sit and eat at the back seats, which are free; I often choose those seats because it helps me sleep. I tend to repeat the same pattern of flying (same airline, same route, same time) rather than exploring other places, even though I have flown so many times.
