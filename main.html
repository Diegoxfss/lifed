0–5 years (1988–1993)

Early sensitivities, picky eating, limited social interaction.

I walked normally but often on my tiptoes. My mother and Doctor Valeria considered using a corrective boot. My aunt, an evangelical missionary, prayed with her hands on my head. She thought I had demons. My grandfather, from the countryside and poor, called me “gay.”

I hated noise; my parents called me “explosive” for complaining. My sister said I was annoying. I spun until I fell and felt lonely as the youngest, staying out of games. I only went out with my sister and waited for the games to end as she was playing with girls usually dancing and I was sitting waiting.

I attended a small neighborhood school my mother started, where I learned to read and write. Social interactions were minimal. At one point I hit other children after seeing a popular classmate do it, but my mother corrected me.

I vomited from certain foods (soursop, guava, jackfruit, oil smell) and avoided foods by texture (coconut chunks, mango, pineapple, grape, pear, kiwi and many other fruits until nowadays). I don’t like watermelon because of the seeds. I don't know where the seeds are. Same applies for grapes even though it has just 1 seed. until nowadays I don't eat anything I'm not full aware of . I don't eat many things because of seeds. I started eating olives at the age of 30.

5–10 years (1993–1998)

Keyboard, bedroom, advanced studies, asthma episode.

I got a keyboard and taught myself to play, spending countless hours locked in my room practicing—a habit that lasted until I was 18. I made some friends and occasionally played football, but I was rarely picked for teams, so I returned to playing alone.

I had a childhood friend, Adriel, who lived next door, but his best friend was Bruno. They watched Dragon Ball Z, which I didn’t. I missed many common experiences like cartoons, TV, and video games, and participated very little. I stood out for my high grades and often studied material before the teacher even covered it in class.

Around age 10, I remember hearing voices. I had asthma and was once hospitalized after nearly suffocating at the beach. My father calmed me, and we got to the hospital safely. One memory that stands out is when a kid at a local corner shop told me he wished he had the same Portuguese vocabulary as me while we were eating crisps. I felt amazing that day.

At night, the nebuliser was always ready for me. Around 3 a.m., I would wake up and quietly go to the living room to use it. The mist would fill my lungs, easing the tightness in my chest, and only then could I finally drift back to sleep. Those nights felt long and quiet, but the routine gave me a small sense of relief and control.

Sometimes, I would get scared of the toys on my way to the nebuliser and needed to wake one of my parents to stay with me for a few minutes at night. Occasionally, my sister or brother would go instead. Even now, I still hear my mother’s voice saying, “He woke up,” when I wake up at night or early in the morning as she was in the next room. I used to hear this every time I woke up at night with asthma. They used to call it “nervous asthma.”

When I wake up, everything sounds loud—like standing at the front of a concert stage. Over a few minutes, it gradually settles. The same applies to bright white lights. I remember shouting to have the lights turned off and often going to use the nebuliser in the dark because of it.

I was originally left-handed but was taught to use my right hand. Still use my left leg.

10–15 years (1998–2003)

Anxiety and panic; sensory overload; daydreaming; Discman.

Rebellion and anarcho-punk readings shaped much of my early life. A book led me to several other books. At school, played unusual pranks and spoke my mind, which sometimes caused me to lose friends. At Mazarelo School, remember throwing a chair at a kid and leaving a note on the teacher's desk saying “Happy Teacher’s Day,” with “motherfucker” written inside. This landed me in the principal’s office when the teacher saw it. I later transferred to Anglo Líder, where I developed anxiety and panic attacks — sweating, dizziness, and fatigue — and often left before classes began.

I believe the crises started from going to school alone in a noisy city. I preferred certain buses because of the sounds. In 1998, my parents went to Atlanta and brought me a Discman, which I carried daily, even though it was heavy, to block out street noise. I listened to music until the teacher entered the classroom.

The school administration referred me to a psychologist for testing. She showed me pictures of butterflies, lions, and other images. To this day, don’t really understand what the purpose was, and I haven’t researched it yet. I ended up failing an entire year of school due to anxiety and panic attacks, as I was absent for long periods. Classmates called me “tourist” or “the mute kid.” I often daydreamed; when I returned to reality, someone would laugh. Crises could happen anywhere: on the street, bus, underground, mall, or even while eating with family. I sought calm places, usually the bathroom.

I was asked by the school administration to transfer to another school. My mother refused, arguing that she had three children enrolled there and would have to remove all three. It was an inexpensive, yet still private, school. I had an episode where I told a history teacher(Rose) to f.off in front of the class. But the class was really loud I think I was really stressed on that day. The situation was: at the end of break time, my classmates were being loud, and the teacher entered the room shouting even louder at the class to be quiet. Then I shouted back at her.

Triggers included unwanted long conversations, noise, and sensory overload. During some crises, I heard voices of my parents from my childhood asthma episodes, though they never harmed me. developed excellent ear training by playing music by ear and never learned to read sheet music.

15–20 years (2003–2008)

United States, intense work, depression, conversion.

I went to the U.S. with a bank loan (my father always reminded me I had to repay). My parents worked a lot and were absent; they didn’t know my preferences. They shouted my name; I said it was high, they denied it.

Upon arrival, I lived with my older sister, got an SSN, and used someone’s green card to create documents as if I were 18. worked at Chick-fil-A in Atlanta (bathroom cleaning, then cashier and assistant). The work was simple but required speaking a lot; I faced complaints due to language and pace. I left frustrated once. They sell combos so I memorized it which made my life there easier.

repaid the loan, supported myself studying and working. used drugs before knowing I needed medication, leading to severe depression and daily suicidal thoughts. attended a church (Vine Church Atlanta — Pastor Napoleon), played keyboard, and converted; I remain Christian. I finished high school and returned to Brazil due to depression. At 19, a psychiatrist prescribed clonazepam and fluoxetine.

20–25 years (2008–2013)

Rigid routine, social isolation, jobs, and engagement.

Back in Brazil at 19, I had difficulty adapting; I stayed locked in my room, keyboard from the U.S., chats, and alcohol. Continued with psychiatrist (clonazepam and fluoxetine); helped a bit but remained anxious, isolated, and exhausted.

I focused on studies, tried jobs; social demands were challenging. Routines had to be well-structured; I developed rigid, repetitive organization to manage anxiety. Worked as administrative assistant at Davilla Hospital (was fired) and Arts do Imaginário (called “weird” — I joked but took literally and handled poorly).

I had a girlfriend and engagement for 5 years; at the end, she said she never loved me. She called me “Bob” (from daydream cartoon) and “crazy” weekly. She wanted many outings (malls, shows), where I felt extremely uncomfortable due to noise, crowds, and information overload. After breakup, she commented on my sexual performance and that I overanalyzed. I have a problem dressing up according to the weather.

25–30 years (2013–2018)

Night hotel work, dismissal in 2015, trip to the United Kingdom.

I stayed at home a lot. I worked at a hotel as a night auditor (documents, invoices, payments). I became a night supervisor; few arrivals/departures — bureaucratic profile. I was dismissed in 2015 after the hotel was sold and conflicts with the new management.

I always wanted to go to London. I decided to go (either that or end my life after so many losses). I came with my Portuguese brother-in-law (I have Portuguese citizenship since childhood; at the time the UK was in the EU). Adaptation was very difficult: alcohol, frustrations, feelings, suicidal thoughts.

I didn’t eat much, didn’t know the food, and found the diversity and the noise of the underground strange.

In 2018, friends organized the 'Contradiction Party', a gathering where I was the theme of the celebration. I was no longer living in Brazil, as I had moved to the UK in 2016, and I only found out about it through a photo someone sent to me. I consider many variables at the same time and may change my opinion as new information emerges. This shows again how people view me as different everywhere I go. I know there is something wrong, just don't know what.

30–35 years (2018–2023+)

Routine in London, Whole Foods, software course, British citizenship.

For years I only used the bus because it was quieter; I took the underground only 3 years after moving (noise, crowds, heat). Always with headphones. I bought a motorcycle for a few months, but sold it due to the chaos.

Repetitive diet (usually one meal per day). I kept using headphones. I took a day trip to Brighton following a friend on a motorcycle (major achievement, me on a 125cc with an L plate).

I worked nights at Whole Foods Market. Changes due to poor sales led to layoffs; I switched to day shifts, which was difficult. I opted for a £13,000 redundancy package and left.

Then, a hotel (Danubius Regents Park) using Opera. Before leaving Whole Foods, the government funded a software development course (Tech Talent Academy, UK). Since then, my main focus has been programming and Rubik's cubes.

The hotel provided lodging; I left in less than a year and returned to Whole Foods at the end of 2024. I still struggle to adapt to daytime work, with lots of talking and small talk - depending on the talk. I usually help the guests with excellence and then pretend to do something else in order to make them go away. Some guests have good topics and the opposite happens. I get interested. . Sometimes I ask to leave early. I have reorganized departments (sales increased ~6x), received awards and bonuses; I get emails from leadership, but didn’t take a position due to social demands and my direct communication style.

I am creating my own company (coding and logistics) to work online. I own a flat in Lisbon; I made money in stocks. I became a British citizen in August 2025; the public ceremony caused strong anxiety (front row for hours).

Sensory sensitivities

Sounds, light, touch, startle, linguistic distractions.

Sensitive to many sounds/stimuli: morning magpie, white light,underground, sirens, loud Italian neighbors, birds, and distant conversations. Dislike of physical touch (e.g., shoulder taps). Shower and toilet noises can sound like a waterfall; sometimes I just watch/listen. I notice details (e.g., rats) that others don’t.

My good hearing both helps and distracts: if I hear a familiar language nearby, I can daydream and need to breathe and refocus. I speak Portuguese, Spanish, and English; I understand a bit of French and Italian. I get startled frequently; on flights (Lisbon roughly every 2 months), I nap and still get startled. Today I was startled by a fly returning from Tesco (headphones in pocket). My room faces the backyard and noises coming from there wake me up during the night. In order to avoid waking up I leave the cooling fan on, ambient music and the socket yellow light on at all times. It stays like that even during the daytime. I don't have a keyboard at the moment. That one was a small gift from my mother and I kept it of course.

I take pictures of the sky and the sun every time. I can’t sit with others in the break room due to the smell of food. My rooms are dark, lit only by small socket lights.
